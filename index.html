🌎 Chad Byars — Deep Ancestry Analysis

Beyond "European" — The Ancient Peoples in Your DNA
The Non-European DNA in Chad's Genome
🧬 Neanderthal Heritage — ~2% of Your Genome
Every person of non-African descent carries approximately 1.5–2.5% Neanderthal DNA — the legacy of interbreeding between modern humans and Neanderthals roughly 50,000–65,000 years ago in the Middle East and Europe. This is not a small number: ~2% of a 3-billion-base-pair genome means approximately 60 million base pairs of Neanderthal sequence living in Chad's cells right now.

Neanderthal DNA contributes to immune function (HLA variants), skin and hair keratin production, pain sensitivity, and potentially even sleep chronotype. Some of Chad's immune variants — particularly HLA region diversity — may trace back to Neanderthal introgression that provided resistance to ancient European pathogens. The MC1R red hair variants, while not directly Neanderthal-derived, are part of the same evolutionary story of adaptation to low-UV Northern European environments that both Neanderthals and modern humans independently evolved.
🌌 Yamnaya Steppe Pastoralists — ~50% of European Ancestry
Roughly 5,000 years ago, nomadic herders from the Pontic-Caspian Steppe (modern Ukraine/Russia) swept westward into Europe in one of the largest genetic replacement events in human history. These Yamnaya people brought Indo-European languages (the ancestor of English, Irish Gaelic, and most European languages), horse domestication, wheeled vehicles, and the lactase persistence allele.

Chad's lactase persistence (rs4988235 AA) is a direct genetic signature of Yamnaya ancestry. The ability to digest milk into adulthood was a massive survival advantage for pastoral peoples and spread rapidly. His blue eyes may also trace partially to Yamnaya populations, though the blue-eye mutation (OCA2/HERC2) originated even earlier among Mesolithic hunter-gatherers around the Baltic ~10,000 years ago.

The Yamnaya were not "European" in the modern sense — they were Central Asian steppe nomads who mixed with indigenous European hunter-gatherers and Anatolian farmers to create the modern European gene pool. Roughly half of Chad's genome traces to these non-European steppe origins.
🌾 Anatolian Farmers — ~30% of European Ancestry
Beginning around 9,000 years ago, farming peoples from Anatolia (modern Turkey) and the Fertile Crescent migrated into Europe, bringing agriculture, pottery, and settled village life. These people were genetically distinct from both the indigenous European hunter-gatherers and the later Yamnaya steppe peoples.

Anatolian farmer DNA likely contributes roughly 25–35% of Chad's genome. These Near Eastern / Middle Eastern ancestors brought genes related to grain metabolism, certain immune adaptations from living in dense agricultural settlements, and SLC24A5 — the skin lightening variant that Chad carries (rs1426654 AA). This mutation actually originated in the Near East and spread into Europe with the farmers, meaning Chad's "European skin color" gene is actually of Middle Eastern origin.

The MTHFR variants Chad carries may also trace to this agricultural wave — folate metabolism changes were under strong selection in populations transitioning from diverse hunter-gatherer diets to grain-heavy agricultural diets.
🌊 Western Hunter-Gatherers — ~20% of European Ancestry
Before the farmers arrived from the Middle East and the Yamnaya rode in from the steppe, Europe was home to Mesolithic hunter-gatherers — dark-skinned, blue-eyed peoples who had lived there for over 30,000 years since the last Ice Age.

These ancient Europeans are the source of the blue eye mutation. A single individual near the Baltic Sea around 10,000 years ago carried the first OCA2/HERC2 rs12913832 AA mutation that Chad carries today. That person had dark skin and blue eyes — a combination that seems strange today but was the original European phenotype. When Chad looks in the mirror and sees blue eyes, he is seeing a 10,000-year-old hunter-gatherer mutation from the forests of Mesolithic Europe.

Western Hunter-Gatherer DNA also likely contributes to immune system diversity and potentially to the MC1R variants — as these populations adapted to the low-UV environment of Ice Age Europe over tens of thousands of years.
🌍 Out of Africa — Every Human's Origin
Every base pair in Chad's genome ultimately traces to East Africa, roughly 70,000–100,000 years ago. A small population of anatomically modern humans — perhaps as few as 1,000–10,000 individuals — left Africa and eventually populated the entire non-African world. Every non-African human alive today descends from this founding population.

The genetic diversity within Africa is greater than the genetic diversity between all non-African populations combined. In the grand sweep of human genetic history, the differences between "European," "Asian," and other continental populations represent very recent, very small variations on the African genome. Chad's genome is fundamentally an African genome with ~70,000 years of accumulated adaptations to Northern European environments — lighter skin, blue eyes, lactose tolerance, red-hair-associated MC1R variants — layered on top of the original human blueprint.

🤔 Why Quinn and Not Chad?

The Central Question — Both Carry B*51, Only One Is Symptomatic
The Paradox
Chad carries at least one HLA-B*51 allele. He is an ER physician in his 40s with no Behçet's symptoms. Quinn is B*51 homozygous and is experiencing a health crisis. Why?

This is not unusual — HLA-B*51 is necessary but not sufficient for Behçet's disease. Only ~5-10% of B*51 carriers ever develop BD. The answer lies in the modifier gene constellation, gene dosage, environmental triggers, and maternal genetic contribution.
Hypothesis 1: Gene Dosage Effect
B*51 Homozygous vs. Heterozygous — The Dose Makes the Disease
Chad is B*51 heterozygous (one copy). Quinn is B*51/B*51 homozygous (two copies). Takeuchi et al. (PNAS 2014) demonstrated a gene-dose effect: homozygotes carry OR ~10.96× vs ~5.78× for heterozygotes. Quinn presents nearly double the HLA-mediated antigen presentation of aberrant peptides compared to Chad. This alone may be the primary explanation. Two copies means twice the B*51 molecules on every antigen-presenting cell in Quinn's body, each one capable of triggering the autoinflammatory cascade.
Hypothesis 2: Brigitte's Modifier Genes Compound the Load
The Maternal Contribution We Can't See Yet
Chad contributes one B*51 allele plus his modifier genes (IL-6 CC, CTLA4 AG, STAT4 GT, TYK2 AC, FUT2 AA, etc.) — each with 50% transmission probability. Brigitte contributes the other B*51 plus HER modifier genes. If Brigitte independently carries pro-inflammatory variants (her own IL-6, IL-10, STAT4, ERAP1 alleles), Quinn could have inherited a double dose of modifiers from both parents while Chad only carries his single set.

This is the critical unknown. When Brigitte's 23andMe data arrives, we can determine: did Quinn inherit pro-inflammatory modifiers from BOTH sides? That would explain why he crossed the disease threshold while Chad, with only one set of modifiers, did not.

Specific scenario: If Brigitte also carries IL-6 CC, Quinn could be IL-6 CC/CC homozygous — maximum inflammatory baseline. If she carries ERAP1 risk variants, the epistatic B*51/ERAP1 interaction could be fully loaded in Quinn but absent in Chad (if Chad doesn't carry ERAP1 risk alleles).
Hypothesis 3: ERAP1 Epistasis
The Missing Piece — ERAP1 May Be the Trigger Chad Lacks
ERAP1 trims peptides to the correct length for HLA class I loading. The B*51/ERAP1 epistatic interaction is one of the strongest gene-gene interactions in all of autoimmunity (parallel to B*27/ERAP1 in ankylosing spondylitis). If Quinn inherited ERAP1 risk alleles (from either parent) but Chad does NOT carry them, this would precisely explain the discordance: Chad's B*51 molecules present normal-length peptides (no disease), while Quinn's B*51 molecules present ERAP1-trimmed aberrant peptides (triggering autoimmunity).

This is the single most important gene to test in both Chad and Quinn. If Chad lacks ERAP1 risk alleles and Quinn carries them (from Brigitte), the mechanism is identified.
Hypothesis 4: Environmental Trigger Differential
Age, Microbiome, Stress, and Infection History
Behçet's typically manifests in the 20s-30s. Chad may have passed through the highest-risk age window without sufficient environmental triggering. Potential triggers that Quinn may have encountered but Chad may not have (or may have encountered at a less vulnerable age):

• Streptococcal or HSV infection — molecular mimicry between microbial peptides and B*51-presented self-peptides is a leading BD trigger hypothesis
• Gut microbiome state — if Quinn inherited FUT2 non-secretor + had different antibiotic/dietary exposures, his gut dysbiosis may differ from Chad's
• Psychological stress — chronic stress upregulates Th17 responses, the exact immune axis B*51 drives in BD
• Dietary factors — high-sugar, low-fiber Western diet reduces butyrate-producing bacteria, which are already depleted in BD
• Sleep disruption — chronic sleep debt impairs regulatory T-cell function, weakening the immune brakes that keep B*51 in check
Hypothesis 5: Epigenetic Differences
Same DNA, Different Expression
Even if Quinn and Chad carried identical modifiers (they don't — Quinn has two B*51 copies), epigenetic modifications (DNA methylation, histone acetylation) can silence or amplify gene expression without changing the sequence. Environmental exposures, diet, stress, and even in utero conditions modify the epigenome. Quinn's B*51 locus may be more actively transcribed than Chad's due to epigenetic differences accumulated over their different lifetimes. MTHFR compound heterozygous status (if inherited) directly affects methylation capacity — the very machinery that controls epigenetic gene regulation.
The Verdict: Most Likely Explanation
Gene Dosage + Maternal Modifiers + Possible ERAP1
The most parsimonious explanation is a three-hit model:

Hit 1: B*51 gene dosage. Quinn has TWO copies where Chad has one. This alone nearly doubles the OR (10.96× vs 5.78×).

Hit 2: Maternal modifier gene contribution. Brigitte's genome (arriving soon) likely carries additional pro-inflammatory variants that compound Chad's already-present modifiers. Quinn may have inherited inflammatory alleles from BOTH parents while Chad only carries his own set.

Hit 3: ERAP1 and/or environmental trigger. ERAP1 risk alleles from Brigitte (if present) would complete the molecular mechanism. An environmental trigger (infection, stress, microbiome shift) then ignites the loaded gun.

The 21-Year-Old Control Subject
The brother is a natural experiment. Three outcomes are possible:

Scenario A: Brother is B*51/B*51 homozygous + no symptoms → environmental trigger is the key differentiator. Look at what Quinn experienced that the brother didn't.
