The Real Talk Version
What's actually wrong with me? (No doctor words)
Your immune system has a glitch. It's supposed to fight infections and leave your own body alone. Yours does the fighting part fine — actually too well — but it also attacks your own mouth, gut, and possibly your blood vessels. That's why you get the ulcers.

Behçet's disease (BD) is the name for this pattern: mouth ulcers + gut inflammation + sometimes eye problems, skin stuff, headaches, blood clots. It's not cancer. It's not contagious. It's your immune system being confused about what to attack. The confusion comes from a gene called HLA-B*51 that you got two copies of (one from each parent). Most people with BD have one copy. You have two. That's why yours hit harder.

The EBV (mono) you got in September was like throwing gasoline on a fire that was already set. The mono didn't CAUSE the underlying problem — but it triggered it. Before September, you were walking around with these genes and nothing was happening. The mono flipped the switch.

2. Cholestyramine Trial — For the Morning Diarrhea
Your October colonoscopy showed terminal ileal inflammation with scarring. The terminal ileum is where your body reabsorbs bile acids. If it's scarred, bile acids dump into your colon every morning instead of being recycled. Bile acids in the colon = watery, urgent morning diarrhea. This is called bile acid malabsorption.

How to test it: Just try cholestyramine (a powder you mix with water) for a few days. If the morning diarrhea improves within 3-5 days, that's your answer. No fancy test needed — the trial IS the test.

This is treatable, fixable, and completely separate from the BD. It's a downstream consequence of the ileal inflammation that nobody's gone back to check for.

The Two Genetic Findings
1. HLA-B*51/B*51 Homozygous — The Autoimmune Driver
OR ~10.96× for Behçet's disease (Takeuchi PNAS 2014). B*51 causes aberrant peptide presentation → CD8+ T cell cross-reactivity → Th17/Treg imbalance → neutrophilic vascular/mucosal inflammation. This explains the ulcers, the colitis pattern, and potentially the migraine cycles (neurovascular inflammation). Two copies means double the signal intensity on every cell. Dad is a carrier (one copy, never symptomatic).

Full analysis: Behçet's Intelligence System
2. UNC13D VUS (MUNC13-4) — The Cytotoxic Defect
Heterozygous c.2782C>T, p.Arg928Cys in exon 29. UNC13D encodes MUNC13-4, a protein essential for NK cell and cytotoxic T lymphocyte degranulation — the mechanism by which immune cells kill virus-infected cells and regulate inflammation. Abnormal NK cell degranulation confirmed (reduced CD107a).

This may explain why EBV triggered such a catastrophic response. Normal NK cells rapidly kill EBV-infected B cells. If Quinn's NK cells can't degranulate efficiently, EBV-infected cells persist → uncontrolled immune activation → cytokine storm → ferritin 7,105, EF 33%, WBC 0.21.

Biallelic UNC13D mutations cause FHL3 (familial HLH type 3). A heterozygous VUS may cause a partial degranulation defect — enough to handle normal infections but overwhelmed by EBV's aggressive B-cell infection. Research agent deployed — data incoming.

Key question: Is there a SECOND hit on the other UNC13D allele? Or another gene in the cytotoxic pathway (PRF1, STX11, STXBP2, LYST, RAB27A)? Full HLH gene panel and/or whole exome sequencing should be discussed with genetics.
CRITICAL: The ClinVar Paradox — Benign Variant, Abnormal Function
ClinVar classifies Quinn's UNC13D c.2782C>T (p.Arg928Cys) as BENIGN (2-star, multiple submitters, no conflicts). Two independent labs classified it as benign. BUT Quinn's CD107a NK cell degranulation IS abnormal.

This discordance has three possible explanations:

1. There is a SECOND HIT hiding. Standard gene panels may miss deep intronic UNC13D variants — the 253-kb inversion and intronic hotspots (c.118-308C>T, c.118-307G>A) are common in Northern Europeans and explained 25 additional patients in one North American study. Or there may be a digenic variant in another degranulation pathway gene (PRF1, STX11, STXBP2, RAB27A). A 2021 review documented 44 patients with digenic HLH variants where two heterozygous variants in different genes combined to cause clinical disease.

2. The ClinVar classification may not apply in this clinical context. A 2025 pedigree study showed a monoallelic UNC13D variant could partially impair NK function in adult-onset EBV-HLH. "Benign" in population frequency doesn't mean "benign" when combined with B*51 homozygosity and EBV challenge.

3. The reduced CD107a was reactive (transient). Inflammation can transiently reduce CD107a. The test MUST be repeated when Quinn is well to determine if the defect is constitutional. If it normalizes when healthy, the acute inflammatory state was the cause. If it remains low, there IS a genetic degranulation defect that standard sequencing hasn't found yet.

EBV-positive mucocutaneous ulcer (EBVMCU) is a WHO-recognized clinicopathologic entity (2017 WHO classification).
