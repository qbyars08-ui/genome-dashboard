What We're Fighting
Behçet's Disease — In Plain English
Your immune system has two copies of a gene called HLA-B*51 that sits on the surface of every cell in your body. Its job is to show pieces of proteins to your immune system's patrol cells so they can check if anything is wrong. The problem is that B*51 sometimes shows pieces of YOUR OWN proteins that look similar to pieces of bacteria or viruses you've been exposed to. Your immune system can't tell the difference — so it attacks your own body, thinking it's fighting an infection.

This attack happens primarily in blood vessels — which is why Behçet's can cause ulcers in your mouth (tiny blood vessels in the lining), skin problems, eye inflammation, headaches, joint pain, and in some cases blood clots. The blood vessels in different organs are the common target.

Having TWO copies of B*51 (instead of one like your dad) means every cell in your body is showing twice as many of these confusing protein pieces. That's why you crossed the threshold into symptoms while your dad, who has only one copy, never did.

The good news: We know what's driving it. We know which genes are involved. We know what makes it better and what makes it worse. And we have a plan.
